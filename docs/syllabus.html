Course Description. This course is an introduction to mathematical proof, with examples from combinatorics, number theory, set theory, and calculus.

Textbook: The Book of Proof, by Richard Hammack. This is an open textbook that is available for download at Hammack’s Website. You can also purchase a paperback version of the book if you wish, but that is not required.

Prerequisites. MATH 1132 or 1152. Cannot be taken for credit after passing MATH 2143, 3150, 3210, 3230, 3240, 3260, 3270, 3330, 3370 or 224. Note that a grade of C or better in this course is required for the Mathematics major and is a prerequisite for many of the 3000 level courses in the department.

Class operation: This is a modified "flipped" class, with the course materials online and class time devoted to discussion of homework.   1. I will provide video discussions of key topics from the syllabus and will expect you to watch those videos and do the assigned reading, and work on the assigned problems outside of class.

  2. Class periods will be devoted to small group discussion of problems and writing samples. I have assigned each of you to a group of 5 or 6 people with a designated 15 minute window during each of the two class periods per week. The group assignments are available on HuskyCT. During these brief conferences we will discuss homework problems. I will assign a selection of problems for these group discussions. NOTE: Participation in these discussions counts as \(25\%\) of your grade in the course!

Assessment. Grades in this course will be based on the following:

  • A take-home midterm exam, worth \(25\%\) of the total grade. This will be (tentatively) be distributed on March 5 with a due date of March 13.

  • A take-home final exam worth \(40\%\) of the final grade.

  • You are required to attend and participate in the small group discussions. Your active participation in these discussions, by doing the required prep work and contributing to the discussion, is worth \(25\%\) of your course grade.

  • Online assessments and periodic writing assignments will contribute an additional \(10\%\) of your grade.
